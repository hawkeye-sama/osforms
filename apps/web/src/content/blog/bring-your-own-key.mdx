If you've used a form backend before, you've hit this wall: you want to forward submissions to your email, or append them to a Google Sheet, and the service locks that behind a $9–$15/month plan.

Here's the part that should bother you: **those integrations run on your API keys**.

You provide the Resend key. You grant the Google OAuth token. The BYOK form backend just makes the API call. But they charge you for the privilege of using your own credentials.

That's the problem osforms is built to solve.

## What BYOK Means

BYOK — Bring Your Own Keys — means you supply the API credentials, and the platform uses them to execute integrations on your behalf.

This is already how most things work at the infrastructure level. You bring AWS credentials to Terraform. You bring a Stripe secret key to your payments library. The tool doesn't charge you for Stripe usage — Stripe does.

Form backends should work the same way. If you're paying Resend $0.10 per 1,000 emails, why is the form backend charging you again just to call the Resend API?

> "You're not paying for integrations. You're paying for the markup on your own API keys."

## How osforms Implements BYOK

When you add an integration in osforms:

1. You paste your API key (Resend, webhook signing secret, etc.) or complete an OAuth flow (Google Sheets)
2. osforms encrypts the credential at rest with AES-256-GCM using a per-installation encryption key
3. When a submission arrives, osforms decrypts the credential in memory and executes the integration
4. The decrypted key is never logged or persisted beyond the execution

```
Submission → osforms → decrypts your key → calls Resend API → logs result
                                                 ↑
                                     Resend bills your account
```

Your key, your data, your costs. The BYOK form backend model means your provider costs accrue to you directly — not through an intermediary with a margin on top.

## Security Considerations

Storing third-party credentials introduces real responsibility. Here's exactly what osforms does:

**Encryption at rest**: Every API key is encrypted with AES-256-GCM before hitting the database. The encryption key lives in the environment, not the database. If someone dumps your MongoDB, they get ciphertext.

**Never logged**: Decrypted credentials are used in memory and discarded. They don't appear in logs, error messages, or any persistent storage.

**Scoped OAuth**: The Google Sheets integration requests only `drive.file` scope — access to files you explicitly designate, not your entire Drive.

> "The code is open source. You don't have to trust our description of how your keys are handled — you can read the implementation."

## Open Source as a Trust Signal

osforms is fully open source on [GitHub](https://github.com/hawkeye-sama/osforms). You can audit exactly how credentials are stored, encrypted, and used. There's no black box.

If you want to self-host — run osforms on your own infrastructure with your own MongoDB — you can. The same codebase that powers the hosted version is the codebase you can inspect, fork, and deploy yourself.

This matters for teams with strict data policies. If you can't send form submissions through a third-party service, run osforms yourself. That's not a theoretical option; it's how the project is designed.

## The osforms Take

We didn't build osforms to compete on features. We built it because the pricing model of existing form backends is backwards.

When you supply the integration credentials, the integration cost should accrue to your provider account — not to the form backend's margin. Charging for integrations in this model is a tax on developer convenience, not a reflection of the value delivered.

The BYOK form backend model isn't novel — it's just honest. Infrastructure tooling has worked this way for years. Form backends adopted the SaaS playbook instead: lock integrations behind paid tiers, call them "advanced features," and collect the delta between your API key's actual cost and what developers will tolerate paying.

osforms exists because that model is worth refusing. The integrations are free because they cost us nothing to provide — you bring the key, you bear the provider cost. Our only legitimate revenue is on submission volume, which scales with actual usage.

## FAQ

**What integrations does osforms support?**
Currently: Resend (email notifications), Google Sheets (row append on each submission), and custom webhooks with HMAC-SHA256 signatures. All three run on your credentials. All three are free.

**How does osforms make money without charging for integrations?**
Submission volume. The free tier is 100 submissions/month. Heavy users can upgrade. The integrations are never gated — only the volume.

**Can I really self-host the entire thing?**
Yes. Clone the repo, point it at your own MongoDB, set your environment variables, deploy anywhere. The docs cover self-hosting. Nothing in the codebase requires the hosted version.

**How is the credential encryption key managed?**
It lives in the `ENCRYPTION_KEY` environment variable — 64 hex characters (32 bytes). You generate it; we never see it. In the hosted version, it's a deployment secret. In self-hosted, it's yours entirely.

**What happens if I delete my osforms account?**
All submissions and encrypted credentials are deleted. There's no vendor lock-in on your data because the data format is standard JSON and you can export at any time before deletion.

**Is this different from just building a serverless function to call Resend?**
Yes, in two ways. First, you'd still need to write, deploy, and maintain that function. osforms replaces the function — you just change your form's `action` attribute. Second, osforms gives you submission storage, a dashboard, integration logs, and spam protection as part of the deal. A single serverless function gives you one email per submission with no audit trail.
